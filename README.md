## What Is This?

In **357 CE**, a woman named **Su Hui** (蘇蕙) wove a poem into silk brocade to win back her exiled husband. Not just any poem—a **29×29 grid of 841 Chinese characters** that can be read in any direction to produce **over 7,958 distinct rhyming poems**.

This repository contains:
- **The first complete English translation** of all 841 characters
- **An interactive web app** to explore the grid and extract poems in real-time
- **Scholarly analysis** of reading patterns and embedded structures

### The Story

**Su Hui** was married to **Dou Tao**, a governor of the Former Qin dynasty. When he was exiled to the frontier and took a concubine, Su Hui—heartbroken but brilliant—spent months weaving an impossible poem into silk brocade with five-colored thread.

She sent it to him.

The poem could be read **forward, backward, up, down, diagonally, in spirals**—every path revealing new verses of longing, devotion, and pain. Dou Tao was so moved he divorced the concubine and returned to her.

The poem survived. Their love story became legend.

## Reading Directions

The genius of the Star Gauge is its **multi-directional readability**. Every path through the grid produces coherent, rhyming poetry:

```
        READING PATTERNS
        ════════════════

          → Horizontal (forward)          ⤡ Diagonal (NE to SW)
          ← Horizontal (reverse)          ⤢ Diagonal (NW to SE)
          ↓ Vertical (down)               ⟳ Circular (border, clockwise)
          ↑ Vertical (up)                 ⟲ Circular (border, counter-clockwise)

        + Skip patterns, spiral patterns, and grid-section readings
```

### The Border Poem

The **outer ring of 112 characters** forms a single circular poem—readable in both directions:

**Clockwise:**
> 仁智怀德圣虞唐，贞志笃终誓穹苍，钦所感想妄淫荒，心忧增慕怀惨伤
>
> *"Benevolence and wisdom cherish virtue—sages Shun and Yao.*
> *Chaste will sincere to the end, swearing to the vault of heaven.*
> *Revering what I feel—vain, lustful, desolate.*
> *Heart's grief grows in yearning, cherishing miserable wounds."*

**Counter-clockwise:**
> 伤惨怀慕增忧心，荒淫妄想感所钦，苍穹誓终笃志贞，唐虞圣德怀智仁
>
> *"Wounded and miserable, cherishing yearning, worry in my heart grows.*
> *Desolate licentiousness, vain thoughts—feeling what I revere.*
> *Vault of heaven, swearing to the end, sincere will.*
> *Yao and Shun—sage virtue cherishing wisdom and benevolence."*

## Translation Notes

### Critical Correction: 唐 ≠ Tang Dynasty

You'll notice the character **唐** (táng) appears in the poem. This does **NOT** refer to the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE)—which wouldn't exist for another 260 years after Su Hui wrote this.

In classical Chinese, **唐虞** (Táng-Yú) or **虞唐** (Yú-Táng) refers to the legendary sage-emperors:

| Character | Emperor | Era |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| 唐 (Táng) | **Yao** (堯) | ~2356-2255 BCE |
| 虞 (Yú) | **Shun** (舜) | ~2255-2205 BCE |

This is a classical literary stock phrase meaning "the golden age of virtuous rule"—China's equivalent of referencing King Arthur or the Garden of Eden.

The later Tang Dynasty actually took its name FROM this ancient legendary association.

## What's Lost in Translation

Some things cannot survive translation:

| Lost Element | Why |
|--------------|-----|
| **Rhyme schemes** | Based on Chinese phonology (tones, finals) |
| **Palindrome properties** | Chinese grammar allows reversibility |
| **Tonal musicality** | Four tones create melody impossible in English |
| **Visual symmetry** | Grid structure is semantic in Chinese |
| **Character density** | Each character = 1-5 English words |

We preserve **meaning** and **structure**. The **music** lives only in the original.
