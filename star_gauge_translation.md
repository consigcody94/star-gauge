> **CRITICAL NOTE:** 唐 (táng) here does NOT refer to the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE), which wouldn't exist for another 260+ years. This is a stock literary phrase meaning "the golden age of virtuous rule."

## THE CIRCULAR BORDER POEM

The outer ring of 112 characters (perimeter of 29×29 minus interior) forms a single circular poem that can be read clockwise or counterclockwise.

### Clockwise Reading (starting from top-right corner):
**仁智怀德圣虞唐，贞志笃终誓穹苍，钦所感想妄淫荒，心忧增慕怀惨伤**

*Translation:*
> Benevolence and wisdom cherish virtue—sages Shun and Yao.
> Chaste will, sincere until the end, swearing to the vault of heaven.
> Revering what I feel and think—vain, licentious, desolate.
> Heart's worry grows in yearning, cherishing miserable wounds.

### Counter-clockwise Reading:
**伤惨怀慕增忧心，荒淫妄想感所钦，苍穹誓终笃志贞，唐虞圣德怀智仁**

*Translation:*
> Wounded and miserable, cherishing yearning, worry in my heart grows.
> Desolate licentiousness, vain thoughts—feeling what I revere.
> Vault of heaven, swearing to the end, sincere chaste will.
> Yao and Shun, sage virtue, cherishing wisdom and benevolence.

> **NOTE ON 唐虞 (Táng-Yú):** This classical phrase refers to the legendary sage-emperors Yao (唐堯, r. ~2356-2255 BCE) and Shun (虞舜, r. ~2255-2205 BCE), NOT the Tang Dynasty (618-907 CE). Su Hui wrote circa 357 CE—the Tang Dynasty wouldn't exist for another 261 years. The later Tang Dynasty actually took its name from this ancient legendary association.

## STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS

### The Grid's Architecture

The 29×29 grid is organized into:

1. **Outer Border** (112 characters) - The circular poem

   - Red/Maroon (外围红区) - 32 seven-character phrases

### Reading Patterns

| Pattern | Direction | Produces |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Horizontal forward | → | Poems |
| Horizontal backward | ← | Different poems (palindrome) |
| Vertical down | ↓ | Poems |
| Vertical up | ↑ | Different poems |
| Diagonal NE | ↗ | Poems |
| Diagonal NW | ↖ | Poems |
| Diagonal SE | ↘ | Poems |
| Diagonal SW | ↙ | Poems |
| Circular (border) | ⟳ | Single poem |
| Circular (border) | ⟲ | Different poem |
| Skip patterns | Various | Additional poems |

### Why Translation "Fails"

1. **Phonetic Loss**: Chinese rhyme schemes depend on final sounds (韵母) that don't translate
2. **Tonal Loss**: The four tones create musicality impossible in English
3. **Grammatical Flexibility**: Chinese characters can function as any part of speech
4. **Visual Loss**: The grid's visual symmetry is semantic in Chinese
5. **Density Loss**: Each character carries meaning that requires 1-5 English words

### Historical Significance

Su Hui composed this work circa 357 CE when her husband Dou Tao was exiled to the frontier. Legend says she wove it on silk with five-colored thread and sent it to him. He was so moved that he divorced his concubine and returned to Su Hui.

The work has been studied, analyzed, and expanded for 1,700 years:
- **Wu Zetian** (Tang Empress) extracted 200+ poems and wrote a preface
- **Monk Qizong** (Song Dynasty) found 3,752 poems
- **Kang Wanmin** (Ming Dynasty) developed 12 reading methods, finding 4,206 poems
- Modern scholars using computational methods have found 7,958+ poems
